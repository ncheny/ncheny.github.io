Lunular

Mock AR Nail Design App
The app offers nails customization integrated with augmented reality, users can save their own designs, find salon stores near them, and browse through salon designs that they’ve made through the app. This was a 8-week project focused on creating, evaluating , and prototyping interface designs.
  • TASKS Sketching, user research, prototype building, website front end design
  • TOOLS Figma, Adobe XD, Axure, Adobe Illustrator, Photoshop, HTML
  • TEAM Chris Wong, Samyak Sah, Praneet Atwal
  • Summer 2019

Final Website

Problem

Though some initial research, we found that some businesses are finding it harder to promote their products as users are opting to do more shopping online. These businesses tend to be in the area where customers historically can only go in store to buy the products that would fit the customers’ size, which are retailers in the jewelry, nails, or clothing industry.

We decided to focus on the nails enthusiasts demographic, where many felt that drop-in appointment wait time can be too long, with no clear catalogue of options, or an overwhelming amount of customization choices.

Opportunity

Inspired by recent development in the AR industry, I felt that by incorporating augmented reality into the nail designing process (by projecting customized designs onto the user’s nails captured by their phone camera screen) could help nail enthusiasts make a more informed choice before an expensive appointment with salons.

With the inclusion of AR technology, it could potentially be a quick prototyping tool that can allow users to try out different designs or colour that could match their skin tone or unique style.

Existing Apps

I started to look for other nails customization integrated with augmented reality app that allows users to create their own nail design and test it out in real time using their phone camera.

Some existing application that were found includes Wanna Nail and YouCamNail. Both application that uses similar AR tech, but are limited to imposing color or flat design changes onto a static hand illustration.

The lack of any existing nail design apps that have incorporated AI technology gave us a niche that can allow us to explore and experiment different UI and UX ideas.

Audience

I conducted a handful of interviews with friends who have been decorating their nails for 3 months- 2 years to understand the main user group of our app. I wanted to know what part of nail design that they are passionate about, how much they invest in their nails, what are some pain points, and what they are looking for in an app that can help speed up their process. Apart from this, I also visited 3 nail salons nearby to understand the booking and waiting period of a walk-in appointment.

From this hands-on research we created 2 distinct personas that helped narrow the direction of our target audience: J. Wilson and Glam Salon.

Wireflows

After conducting a few interviews with our target audience, we identified frictions our participants have encountered during and after their nail appointments. The data allowed us to identify areas where our app could enhance user experience, which can be seen in our persona’s journey map (left).
I also began sketching out the navigation wireflows of our app, which consists of 4 main features that users can interact with: a customization feature, saved designs, designs exploration made by different users, and searching for nail salons in the area. Onboarding tutorials in the beginning also introduces each feature to the user so they won’t feel overwhelmed (right).

Prototyping

The main purpose of the project is to creating an interactive prototype of the app. To do this, I experimented with Figma, Adobe XD, and Axure. I had to switch from one tool to the other due to the large amount of animation that would be needed to simulate a creation tool experience. This was hard to accomplish on the former two programs, but with a bit of coding, can be accomplish on Axure. Following this, I conducted 2 out of 5 user testings and compiled the major UX and efficiency issues.

Reframing

Findings

From the user testing feedback, we found that many of our buttons were a bit small for users with long nails to interact with. Thus, some button sizes were made larger. Other changes include naming of the “Tools” was changed to “Design”, as users weren’t accustomed to the former term. Since Axure doesn’t have dragging and dropping, we weren’t able to fully build our customization prototype or the hold to select feature that can be seen on Pinterest, which skewed our data a little bit.

Style Sheet

With a lot of stuff that need fixing, I also made adjustments to the initial layout, grid, and solidified our style guide and app name. The style guide helped maintain consistency of assets created by our team for Lunular.

Cutting Back

A major change that I realized was needed after testing it out, was the large scale of our app. Cutting a feature became neccessary to decrease the complexity of Lunular, so we came to the conclusion that the exploration of designs made by other users may not be our highest priority.

Many nail lovers already follow artists they enjoy on social media to keep up with the trend, or keep Pinterest board that shows all their latest inspirations. With nearly all of our participants saying that they are comfortable searching for their designs through Google, Instagram, or Pinterest, we decided the exploration feature wouldn’t be eliminating any frictions that were in need of solving.

Website

From the feedbacks and design shift, I proceeded to complete the final user interface design for all of the 55 different UI screens, icons, logo, and artifacts. Following the completion of the screens, I conducted internal user tests within the team for any bugs or UX issues. In the final week, I created the website design using Figma. Referencing our style sheet, brand colours, brand motifs, and a rough web layout by P.Atwal, I created 3 webpages for Lunular that showcased its features, our process journey, and a profile of our team. [Website]

Reflection

Although the AR technology isn't quite there to currently allow a clear read on one's finger nail, we hope our project could inspire those who are interested in AR development to realize the multiple benefits it can be for any communities- even one as unlikely as nail art enthusiasts!
